Course Overview

This course is designed to walk a technician through the hydraulic schematics they may see out on the assembly floor or customer plant. Hydraulic systems can change based on the customer need, however, most hydraulic systems use functional circuit blocks. This eases the engineering and development time required to design the systems from scratch. With this course, we will break the hydraulic schematics down by their functional blocks and walk you through what each of these circuits does. Functional blocks are groups of components that will perorm a specific task. Each functional block will be noted by a number that represents where it is in the circuit with block 1 being the start of the circuit. Breaking complex circuits into smaller, more manageable pieces will deepen our understanding of the components and function of the circuit as well as lessen the burden understanding the system making it less intimidating.

Hydraulic Schematics

Hydraulic schematics are hydraulic circuits represented with symbols. Engineers use schematics to communicate the design, components, layout, and function of a hydraulic circuit. Technicians must have a thorough understanding of hydraulic schematics in order to assemble and troubleshoot effectively. Most press models come with separate schematics for hydraulic, pneumatic, lubrication, and electrical systems. For less complicated systems, you may find schematics that incorporate more than one type of system. This speeds up the development time and greatly improves troubleshooting. For simplicity of more complex circuits, you will generally find the systems have been separated into their respective sources.

Fluid Flow and Pressure

Hydraulic systems require hydraulic pressure to operate. The hydraulic pump in the system transfers energy from the electric motor to the hydraulic fluid. This displacement of fluid and resistance to flow creates pressure. Some of the energy expended by the pump will be converted into pressure while the rest of the energy is converted into flow and heat. Fluids in motion exert less pressure. By causing fluid flow in the hydraulic system at the pump, we create a pressure gradient or differential where atmospheric pressure pushes fluid from the reservoir into the pump inlet. Once the fluid is passed through the pump it is forced through the circuit where it can perform work. Fluids that perform work, such as passing through a restriction or filter, will have a decrease in pressure.

Fluid Conditioning

Fluid conditioning is the act of preparing the hydraulic fluid for use in the system. This could be screening, filtering, and regulating the pressure. Fluid conditioning is critical to the operation and life expectancy of components within the circuit. Most hydraulic systems start with a suction strainer. This strainer will normally remove the larger bits of foreign debris such as metal shavings and lint. Suction strainers are selected based on the pump manufacturers recommendation for volume of flow and maximum debris size that can be safely handled by the pump.

Prep Before Start

Check to make sure the press has been filled with the correct type and volume of hydraulic fluid. The type and amount will normally be listed on the manufacturing notes.

Block 1: Fluid Conditioning and Flow

The bed oil level switch (1) is an optic level 24VDC switch that will close and send an electrical signal back to the PMC when the press has been serviced with the correct volume of hydraulic fluid. Once this switch is closed, the hydraulic motor can be started. Reservoirs are responsible for holding the hydraulic fluid used by the system. They will normally have more fluid than what is required to operate the system. This allows for fluctuations in system demand as well as dispersing additional heat that builds up from the fluid doing work. The strainer (2) is an in-tank submerged type suction line filter that provides 140 micron filtering and 30 GPM flow rate. Suction strainers are typically made of wire mesh and can be cleaned when clogged to extend their service life. The shutoff valve (3) allows you to quickly shut off fluid flow to the hydraulic pump. Shutoff valves are generally provided at the beginning of hydraulic circuits for emergencies and servicing purposes. The hydrauic pump (4) is a variable displacement medium pressure axial piston pump. It has a pressure compensator (PC) built in. We will take a look at this in the next section. Filter FR1 (5) is a 20 micron high pressure filter. A replacement element (6) will be sent out or changed after the press has been manufactured. These high pressure filters are typically made of Microglass II media for longer life and system protection. These cannot be cleaned or back flowed to extend their service life. The style used in this schematic comes with a built-in clog detector. The model called out in this schematic is electrical visual, meaning a light will illuminate and an electric signal will be sent back to the PMC to alert the filter is clogged.

Describe the function of this circuit:

When the bed oil level switch closes, the hydraulic motor can be started. The hydraulic pump which is coupled to the hydraulic motor will begin to operate. This will cause fluid to flow. Oil is drawn from the bed oil reservoir through a strainer where the larger of the debris will be removed. The fluid will then flow through the shutoff valve and into the A port of the hydraulic pump. Since there is a restriction to flow, pressure will rise on the B port side of the hydraulic pump. Pressure Compensator Operation

Hydraulic pressure on the B port side of the hydraulic pump will push the right side of the PC. This will shift the spool once the pressure reaches the set point of the PC. The normally closed port of the PC will now be connected to the pilot port of the variable displacement pump. This fluid pressure will push a plate that is inside the pump, restricting the volume of hydraulic fluid the pump can draw from the reservoir. Once the pressure on the B port side of the hydraulic pump is no longer at or above the set point of the PC, the return spring will push the spool blocking the hydraulic fluid from acting on the pilot operator. The case drain ensures fluid trapped behind the pumps mechanisms as well as the fluid acting on the pilot can return to tank. Fluid trapped behind pumps will heat up and degrade, damage or destroy the seals and component. High Pressure Filter Operation

Hydraulic fluid from port B of the hydraulic pump will flow through port HP1 of the hydraulic filter assembly. The larger debris (down to 140 microns) has been pre-filtered by the strainer. This provides less strain on the finer 20 micron filter, extending its service life. The fluid passing through this filter will be used by components with closer clearances and tolerances than those seen in the pump. Pressure present in port HP1 will press on one side of the pressure differential switch. As fluid is passed through the filter it will drop in pressure. This will produce a difference in pressure between port HP1 and HP2. If the pressure difference becomes too great, the fluid pressure in port HP1 will activate PDS1 signaling the filter is clogged.
